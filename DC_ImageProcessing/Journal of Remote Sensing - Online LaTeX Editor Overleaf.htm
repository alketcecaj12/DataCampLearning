As of November 2020 the COVID-19 second wave pandemics has reached its highest peak and the
data collected from medical swabs is the first ground-truth dataset that we have about its
size. However, there are studies that estimate the real numbers of the pandemics at least ten
times larger than what reported from swabs data \cite{bassi2020observed}. Moreover, there is a
wide range of research showing that human mobility is the primary reason for the spread of the
viruses \cite{10.1371/journal.pmed.0040013} \cite{zhang2017spread} \cite{viboud2006air}. In
this conditions, monitoring and controlling human mobility and contacts means also monitoring
and controlling the spread of COVID-19. This is particularly important for the stability of a
country's national healthcare system, its hospitals and in particular their intensive care
units. In order to have the situation under control, European governments have put in place a
series of contact tracing techniques and measures including contact tracing apps. However,
despite their potential, these tracing methods and apps have shown not to work so well in EU
countries. They seem to work better in Asian countries where cultural approach to these issues
and privacy constraints are much more relaxed than in the EU countries \cite{cha2020asia}. As
an alternative to contact tracing apps, researchers have pointed out the importance of mobile
data (Call Description Records) for controlling the COVID-19 pandemics and evaluating the
effectiveness of control measures such as physical distancing \cite{woody2020projections}
\cite{gao2020mobile}. It is worth noticing however that the CDR data is currently scarcely used
for tracking the COVID-19 spread. Although CDR data value for epidemiological modelling in
general has been proven in a number of cases \cite{oliver2020mobile} privacy issues and in
particular legislation hampers and limits their use.
\subsection{ Occupancy Prediction and Parking Availability}
Occupancy prediction is crucial for any modern urban infrastructure management and as such has
been studied by using many types of data and different prediction methods. For example the
study in \cite{barbour2019planning} introduces a novel approach to occupancy estimation problem
by using a large mobile phone dataset. The authors of \cite{CHEN2019183} instead, have been
using Wi-Fi and Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) sensor data in order to build a system that can
identify the geospatial distribution of occupants and future track or predict their movements.
Other studies like the one in \cite{AngSH17} shows how regression techniques can be applied to
data from carbon dioxide sensors in order to predict human occupancy in buildings. As for the
possible application scenarios in which occupancy prediction can be seen they go from
forecasting the energy consumption for heating or cooling needs like in
\cite{kleiminger2014smart} \cite{peng2018using} to sustainability studies like the approach
presented in \cite{barbour2019planning}.
Inside this range of applications there is also Parking Availability prediction which is about
finding innovative ways of making better use of the limited car parking facilities in the urban
areas. In particular it tries to answer a driver's question: "where do I have the highest
chance to find free parking spaces?". Giving drivers this type of information in real-time is
not easy. For example the work in \cite{XIAO201819} proposes a queuing model combined with a
Markov chain method in order to better consider the variations of arrival and departure in the
parking spaces, the daily patterns, and also the variations during special events. The method
is then validated by using simulated and real world data. The work in \cite{7112165} instead,
propose a multivariate autoregressive model that takes into account both temporal and spatial
correlations of parking availability. The model recommends to drivers parking locations which
have the highest probability of providing at least one parking spot available at the estimated
arrival time. Model's results are ultimately validated by using real-time parking data in the
urban areas of San Francisco and Los Angeles.
Finally, the parking availability problem will be solved by real-time solutions and the
availability of cheap IoT sensors has made this problem easier to face. However the lack of
integration of the sensors data with the car navigators remains challenging. In this sense,
parking availability prediction from historical data has shown to provide valuable solutions.

The importance of crowd sensing1and crowd forecasting is being increasingly recognized, and alarge number of approaches are being proposed. Nevertheless, a comprehensive overview of such arelevant theme is still missing from the literature, and filling this gap is the intended contributionof this paper. In particular, the paper will:•Identify the many application areas in which crowd sensing and prediction can be effectivelyexploited, also reporting about relevant examples from the literature and from the real-world.These include: mobility planning, social planning, event management, crowd steering, andvirus planning.•Overview the many sensing technologies that have the potential to be useful towards crowdsensing, and characterize the type of data one can obtain from them. These technologiesinclude mobile phones, Wi-Fi antennas, social networks, and surveillance cameras.•Analyze the many forecasting methods that have been proposed in the literature, their extent ofapplicability, and their effectiveness. Indeed, those methods are based on different algorithmicapproaches and cannot disregard the deployment of data generating technologies. Moreover,the specific application scenario together with the forecasting need of “how many hours/daysahead” we should forecast, may also be a determinant for the approach taken for the forecastingprocess.Finally, the paper tries to identify some relevant open research challenges in the area of crowdsensing and prediction.The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. In particular, in Section 2 we overview themany application areas of crowd sensing and forecasting. In Section 3 we present the possible datasources that can be used to model crowd distribution and dynamics. Section 4 presents overviewtechniques for crowd forecasting.
